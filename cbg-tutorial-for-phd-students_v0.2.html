An invitation for entry-level PhD students. Three perspectives, one construction.

0. How to read this document

This is a tutorial summary of two recent papers:

Letter. A. Colla, H.-P. Breuer, G. Gasbarri, Unveiling coherent dynamics in non-Markovian open quantum systems: Exact expression and recursive perturbation expansion, Phys. Rev. A 112, L050203 (2025).

Companion. A. Colla, H.-P. Breuer, G. Gasbarri, Recursive perturbation approach to time-convolutionless master equations: Explicit construction of generalized Lindblad generators for arbitrary open systems, Phys. Rev. A 112, 052222 (2025).

The two papers are companion pieces. The Letter announces the result and applies it; the Companion derives the underlying construction and computes the explicit perturbative expansion to fourth order. Read in that order if you only want the headline; read in the reverse order if you want to follow the derivation.

This summary is written for the moment in your PhD when you have just learned the Lindblad master equation, you have heard the words non-Markovian and strong coupling in seminars, and you are starting to ask: what is the actual object that an experimentalist measures, what is the actual object that a theorist computes, and why don’t they always seem to be the same thing?

The CBG papers offer a precise answer to that question — not the only answer, but a clean one. This document walks you through what the answer is, why it matters, and where each of the three communities (experiment, theory, numerics) plug in.

A note on register. I will sometimes say “we” when describing what the construction does. That “we” is not the CBG authors and not me; it is you and the construction working together. This is a tutorial; you are the one driving.

1. The setting: open quantum systems, in three sentences

A closed quantum system evolves unitarily under the Schrödinger equation. An open quantum system is a piece of a larger closed system, where you only have access to (or only care about) a few degrees of freedom — the system — and the rest is the environment or bath. Because you have traced out the environment, the system’s reduced density matrix \(\rho_S(t)\) does not in general evolve unitarily; it can decohere, dissipate, equilibrate, and exhibit memory effects.

The master equation is the equation of motion for \(\rho_S(t)\). The simplest one is the Lindblad (GKLS) form:

\[ \dot\rho_S = -i [H_S, \rho_S] + \sum_k \gamma_k \left( L_k \rho_S L_k^\dagger - \tfrac{1}{2} \{ L_k^\dagger L_k,\, \rho_S \}\right) \]

with positive rates \(\gamma_k\). This is the equation you usually meet first. As an equation it is mathematically self-standing; it does not, by itself, require any approximation. In the textbook microscopic derivation, however, this form usually arises under weak-coupling and Markov approximations (the so-called Born–Markov approximation), with a clear separation of timescales between system and bath. The Lamb shift, dressed atomic levels, spontaneous emission rates — all of these you can extract from the Markovian limit of the Lindblad equation.

The trouble starts when those approximations fail.

2. The trouble: why non-Markovian dynamics is hard

Suppose the bath has internal structure on timescales comparable to the system’s own dynamics. Or suppose the coupling is strong enough that you cannot truncate the system–bath interaction perturbatively. Three things go wrong simultaneously:

  1. Memory. The reduced dynamics is no longer a semigroup. \(\rho_S(t)\) at time \(t\) depends not only on \(\rho_S(t-dt)\) but on the system’s history — information that has flowed into the environment and can flow back.
  2. Time-dependent rates. If you insist on writing a master equation in Lindblad form, the rates \(\gamma_k(t)\) become time-dependent and can even go negative. Negative rates do not, by themselves, mean the dynamics is unphysical — provided the resulting dynamical map remains completely positive on physical states. They are then a signature of non-Markovianity.
  3. Renormalisation. The “Hamiltonian part” of the master equation is no longer just \(H_S\). It is shifted, and the shift is in general time-dependent, not a constant Lamb shift.

The third point is the focus of the CBG papers. To explain why it is subtle, I have to introduce the time-convolutionless (TCL) master equation.

The TCL master equation in one paragraph

The TCL approach replaces the convolution-with-memory-kernel form of the Nakajima–Zwanzig master equation with a time-local generator:

\[ \dot\rho_S(t) = \mathcal{L}_t [ \rho_S(t) ]. \]

The time-locality is real: \(\rho_S(t)\)’s evolution is determined by \(\rho_S\) at the same instant \(t\), not by an integral over its past. The non-Markovian character is hidden in the time-dependence of the generator \(\mathcal{L}_t\) itself. This works whenever the dynamical map \(\Lambda_t\) (the map that takes \(\rho_S(0)\) to \(\rho_S(t)\)) is invertible, which is true in a wide range of physically relevant regimes but is not guaranteed everywhere — there are “TCL singularities” where invertibility fails. (You will hear this referenced often; it is part of the scope.)

Any TCL generator can be written in a generalised Lindblad form:

\[ \mathcal{L}_t [X] = -i [K(t), X] + \sum_{ij} \gamma_{ij}(t) \left( L_i(t)\, X\, L_j^\dagger(t) - \tfrac{1}{2}\{ L_j^\dagger(t) L_i(t),\, X \} \right), \]

where \(K(t)\) is some Hermitian operator and \(\gamma_{ij}(t)\) is a Hermitian (but not necessarily positive) coefficient matrix. This looks just like Lindblad, but with everything time-dependent and with \(\gamma\) allowed to have negative eigenvalues.

Here is the problem the CBG papers solve.

3. The problem: K(t) is not unique

Given a TCL generator \(\mathcal{L}_t\), the decomposition into a “Hamiltonian part” \(K(t)\) and a “dissipator” is not unique. You can shift things around between \(K\) and the dissipator without changing \(\mathcal{L}_t\) itself.

Concretely, the transformations

\[ L_i \to L_i - \alpha_i \mathbb{1}, \qquad K \to K + \frac{1}{2i}\sum_{ij} \gamma_{ij}(\alpha_i L_j^\dagger - \alpha_j^* L_i) \]

leave \(\mathcal{L}_t\) invariant. And there are more. Different choices of \((K, \{L_i\})\) describe the same physical evolution but assign different “Hamiltonians” to it.

This is not a bug. It is the same kind of redundancy you have in gauge field theory, where many vector potentials \(A_\mu\) describe the same physical field strength \(F_{\mu\nu}\). The physics is gauge-invariant; the bookkeeping requires a choice.

So: if there are many \(K(t)\)’s consistent with the same dynamics, which one — if any — should we call “the” effective Hamiltonian of the open system?

This is where the three perspectives matter.

Why each community cares

  • The experimentalist is going to measure something — an energy splitting, an oscillation frequency, a precession rate. They want to know whether the number they extract from a fit corresponds to anything intrinsic about the open system, or whether it is a property of the gauge they implicitly chose by writing the model in some particular way.
  • The theorist wants a principled construction that picks out a canonical \(K(t)\) from the gauge orbit, ideally one with good limiting properties (recovers the Lamb shift in weak coupling, etc.) and one that connects naturally to thermodynamic observables like work and heat in the strong-coupling regime.
  • The numericist wants a computational recipe: given a microscopic model \(H = H_S + H_E + \lambda H_I\), compute \(K(t)\) directly from the model, ideally order by order in some expansion parameter and ideally without having to first solve the full system+bath dynamics exactly.

The CBG construction gives all three communities a common object to work with. It picks a gauge — the Hayden–Sorce minimal-dissipation gauge — which is the theorist’s part; it gives a closed-form operational expression for \(K(t)\) in that gauge, which is the numericist’s part; and it predicts a time-dependent \(K(t)\) whose signatures can be looked for in experiment, which is the experimentalist’s part. It does not, on its own, fully resolve the experimental or thermodynamic interpretation; it gives each community a handle.

4. The Hayden–Sorce gauge fix: minimal dissipation

The gauge fix is from the earlier paper of Hayden and Sorce, A canonical Hamiltonian for open quantum systems, J. Phys. A 55, 225302 (2022). The CBG papers adopt it; you should know it.

The idea is geometrically simple. Among all the \((K, \text{dissipator})\) decompositions of a given \(\mathcal{L}_t\), declare the one for which the dissipator has minimum norm — under a specific Haar-averaged scalar product — to be canonical. Equivalently: choose the unique decomposition in which the jump operators \(L_i\) are traceless.

That tracelessness condition is the practical handle. Lindblad, in his original 1976 paper, already used traceless jump operators in the Markovian semigroup case; the Hayden–Sorce construction extends this convention to arbitrary TCL generators.

Why does “minimum dissipation” feel like the right choice?

  1. Markovian limit. In the weak-coupling Markovian limit, this gauge recovers the textbook Lamb-shifted Hamiltonian — exactly the \(H_S + H_{LS}\) that you would derive from second-order perturbation theory in the system–bath coupling.
  2. Information-theoretic. It is the choice that maximises the average fidelity between \(\mathcal{L}_t\) and a noiseless unitary generator, where the average is over Haar-random pure input states. Loosely: it is the \(K(t)\) that “looks most like a Hamiltonian” by an unbiased measure.
  3. Algebraically clean. Traceless jump operators give a canonical form that is unique once you fix the operator basis.

It is a principled choice. There are competing gauges in the literature — Hamiltonian of mean force in the strong-coupling thermodynamics community; polaron-transformed Hamiltonians in the chemical physics community; Mori-projector approaches. They give different \(K(t)\)’s in general. This is important, and we will return to it.

5. The Letter’s first result: a closed-form expression for K(t)

Now the construction. Suppose you have any TCL generator \(\mathcal{L}_t\) — Hermiticity-preserving, trace-annihilating, on a \(d\)-dimensional system. The CBG Letter shows that the Hayden–Sorce-canonical \(K\) is:

\[ K = \frac{1}{2id} \sum_\alpha \left[ F_\alpha^\dagger,\; \mathcal{L}[F_\alpha] \right] \]

where \(\{F_\alpha\}\) is any Hilbert–Schmidt orthonormal operator basis on the system Hilbert space, i.e. \(\mathrm{Tr}(F_\alpha^\dagger F_\beta) = \delta_{\alpha\beta}\). The result does not depend on which basis you choose — that is the content of “basis-independent.”

In the matrix-unit basis \(F_\alpha = |j\rangle\langle k|\), this is:

\[ K = \frac{1}{2id} \sum_{j,k=1}^{d} \left[ |j\rangle\langle k|,\; \mathcal{L}[|k\rangle\langle j|] \right]. \]

That is the Letter’s Equation (7). It is, in my opinion, the single most useful equation in either paper. Here is why.

What this equation does for each community

  • Numerics. Hand the equation a TCL generator (which you might have computed by HEOM, by TEMPO, by exact diagonalisation of a finite system+bath, by master-equation perturbation theory, or by any other means), and you get \(K(t)\) out by a finite sum. No quadratures. No solving auxiliary equations. The construction is compositional: any way of obtaining \(\mathcal{L}_t\) feeds into the same \(K\) formula.
  • Theory. It says \(K(t)\) is genuinely an attribute of the generator, not a derivation artefact. Two theorists with different microscopic derivations of the same \(\mathcal{L}_t\) will compute the same \(K\) — as they must, since the construction is uniquely fixed by the gauge.
  • Experiment. It tells you what \(K(t)\) is supposed to look like given a model. If you have characterised the dynamics (e.g. by quantum process tomography), you can extract a candidate \(K(t)\) and ask whether the time-dependence you predict matches what is observed in spectroscopy.

A useful sanity check at this point: take the Markovian Lindblad case with constant \(H_S\) and traceless \(L_k\). Plug into Equation (7). You will recover \(K = H_S + H_{LS}\), where \(H_{LS}\) is the standard Lamb shift. The construction passes the textbook limit. Both papers walk through this check.

6. The Companion’s main result: the recursive perturbative expansion

Equation (7) requires you to already know the TCL generator \(\mathcal{L}_t\). In practice, for non-Markovian and strong-coupling problems, you usually don’t. The Companion paper provides the missing piece: a recursive perturbative construction of \(\mathcal{L}_t\) itself, order by order in the system–bath coupling \(\lambda\).

Start with a microscopic Hamiltonian:

\[ H = H_S + H_E + \lambda\, H_I, \qquad H_I = A \otimes B \]

(generalisations to sums of tensor products are straightforward). Assume the initial state factorises: \(\rho_{SE}(0) = \rho_S \otimes \rho_E\). Then in the interaction picture, the reduced dynamical map \(\Lambda_t\) can be Taylor-expanded in \(\lambda\), and the TCL generator can be obtained as

\[ \mathcal{L}_t = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \lambda^n \mathcal{L}_n(t). \]

The Companion’s central technical achievement is a recursion for the time-ordered cumulants of the bath correlation functions (Companion Eq. (27)) that lets you compute \(\mathcal{L}_n\) at any order from the lower orders, without redundant work. The construction preserves the canonical generalised-Lindblad form at every order, so you can read off \(K_n(t)\) and the dissipative part separately, again at every order.

Here is what comes out of running the recursion through fourth order (the Companion does this explicitly in its Section IV):

  • Order 0. Trivial: \(\mathcal{L}_0 = -i[H_S, \cdot]\). The bare system Hamiltonian; no coupling yet.
  • Order 1. Non-zero only if the bath has a non-vanishing first moment, \(\langle B(t) \rangle \neq 0\) — which usually means the bath has initial coherences relative to the interaction. \(K_1\) then describes effective driving on the system.
  • Order 2. This is the rich one. \(K_2\) has a beautiful structure: it splits into a fluctuation piece, governed by the symmetric part of the bath two-point function \(S(t,\tau) = \tfrac{1}{2}\langle\{B(t), B(\tau)\}\rangle - \langle B(t)\rangle\langle B(\tau)\rangle\), and a dissipation piece, governed by the antisymmetric part \(\chi(t,\tau) = i\langle[B(t), B(\tau)]\rangle\). This is a fluctuation–dissipation decomposition of the coherent renormalisation, not of the dissipator. It is one of the Letter’s most striking findings.
  • Orders 3 and 4. Get more elaborate. The Companion provides explicit closed-form expressions; the structural distinction between even and odd orders becomes important and is derived in detail.

If you have ever written a Magnus expansion or a cumulant expansion in stochastic processes, the structure will feel familiar.

7. Two pedagogically clean test cases

The Letter applies the construction to two simple spin systems. They are the cases an entry student should reproduce by hand, because they bring out the main qualitative features cleanly.

Test case A: pure dephasing, A = σ_z

Take \(H_S = (\omega/2)\, \sigma_z\) and couple via \(A = \sigma_z\) to any bath. The structure of the perturbative series simplifies enormously: every odd order of \(K\) is proportional to \(\sigma_z\), every even order vanishes. So \(K(t)\) takes the form

\[ K(t) = \frac{\omega_r(t)}{2}\, \sigma_z \]

with a time-dependent renormalised frequency \(\omega_r(t)\). The eigenbasis of \(H_S\) is not rotated by the interaction; only the level splitting is shifted, and the shift can be time-dependent.

If the bath state happens to be diagonal in the Fock basis — for instance, a thermal state of a linearly coupled bosonic bath — all odd cumulants of \(B\) vanish, and \(K(t) = (\omega/2) \sigma_z\) exactly. No renormalisation. This recovers the well-known result that the dephasing spin-boson model with a thermal bath has no coherent renormalisation, only decoherence.

Test case B: orthogonal coupling, A = σ_x, with a thermal bath

Same \(H_S = (\omega/2)\sigma_z\), but now \(A = \sigma_x\), coupled to a thermal bosonic bath (the standard spin-boson model at finite temperature). Now the parity structure swaps: odd orders of \(K\) vanish, even orders contribute, and they all turn out to be diagonal in \(\sigma_z\).

Result: \(K(t) \propto \sigma_z\), again no eigenbasis rotation, just a time-dependent shift. The Letter proves analytically what was previously only conjectured from numerics (Gatto, Colla, Breuer, Thoss, PRA 110, 032210 (2024)): the spin-boson model at finite temperature does not rotate the qubit’s eigenbasis through the coherent part of its dynamics, regardless of how strong the coupling is.

Why these cases matter pedagogically

These two examples are clean because the algebra collapses. But they also expose the non-trivial prediction: as soon as you have a bath whose correlation functions are not time-symmetric — for instance, a bath with non-vanishing odd cumulants, which can happen for non-thermal bath states or in the presence of coherent driving — the eigenbasis does rotate, and the renormalisation can be much richer.

This is a falsifiable prediction. Find a system where the construction predicts eigenbasis rotation. Look for it in spectroscopy. Get a fingerprint or its absence. Either outcome is informative.

8. Where the three communities meet

Now I can return to the question I posed in §0: what is the actual object that the experimentalist measures, what is the object that the theorist computes, and why don’t they always seem to be the same thing?

The CBG construction provides one principled answer: under the Hayden–Sorce gauge, \(K(t)\) is the well-defined coherent renormalisation, and the construction tells you how to compute it from the microscopic model. Different communities engage with \(K(t)\) differently.

Experimentalist’s view

You measure a frequency \(\omega_{\text{eff}}(t)\), perhaps as a function of how long you let the system interact with its bath, perhaps in some Ramsey or spectroscopic protocol. The CBG construction gives you a prediction for \(\omega_{\text{eff}}(t)\) given a microscopic model — under the Hayden–Sorce gauge.

The Hasse, Palani, Schaetz, Breuer, Warring trapped-ion experiment (Nat. Commun. 16, 2502 (2025)) provides experimental fingerprints of this kind: the paper “provides experimental fingerprints supporting the Ansatz of minimal-dissipation”, in its own words, observing time-dependent energy-level renormalisation in an ultrastrongly coupled open system. The careful word here is fingerprints, not validation: a single experimental observation, especially on a single platform, produces a signature consistent with the prediction. It does not adjudicate uniqueness across competing gauges. Independent fingerprinting on other platforms is exactly the missing piece.

What you can already do: take a system in your lab, identify the relevant \(H_S\), \(H_E\), \(H_I\), compute \(K(t)\) using Equation (7), and compare to spectroscopy. The match or mismatch is an operational test of the model–gauge combination, not a verdict on the framework as such.

Theorist’s view

You have a construction that is mathematically clean: a gauge fix with provable properties, a closed-form expression that is basis-independent, a recursion that builds \(\mathcal{L}_t\) and \(K(t)\) order by order in the coupling. You have a fluctuation–dissipation decomposition of the coherent renormalisation at second order — a result that did not exist before these papers, as far as I am aware.

What you don’t yet have: convergence proofs in the strong-coupling regime, higher-order generalisations of the second-order FDT structure, and adjudication against the competing gauges (HMF, polaron, Mori) in cases where they disagree on physically interesting quantities.

The CBG construction is one coordinate system on a manifold of physically equivalent descriptions. It is a particularly nice coordinate system. It is not the territory.

Numericist’s view

You can implement Equation (7) in twenty lines of code, given a TCL generator from any source. You can implement the recursion Equation (27) in maybe a hundred. You can run sanity checks at every step: basis-independence (compute \(K\) under two different bases, check agreement), parity rules in the spin examples, recovery of the Lamb shift in the weak-coupling Markovian limit. These are structural identity checks that catch numerically-stable-but-wrong implementations.

You can compare against HEOM, TEMPO, MCTDH, or pseudomode simulations of the same model. Each of these methods has different failure modes — hierarchy truncation, memory cutoffs, basis truncations, finite-bath effects — and agreement between methods with non-overlapping failure modes is more informative than agreement between two methods that share the same failure assumptions.

You can also identify failure regimes where the perturbative expansion breaks down. Doing so is not a defect; it is exactly the kind of contribution numerics is uniquely placed to make. A pipeline that delivers a clean failure regime with a cause label (“convergence failure”, “TCL singularity”, “truncation artefact”, “benchmark disagreement”) is more useful than one that only confirms the construction in regimes where everyone already agrees it works.

Where the three meet

The three communities meet at the construction’s interfaces:

  • Theory ↔︎ numerics: The recursive expansion is a recipe. Theory writes it; numerics runs it. The output of running it is a candidate \(K(t)\) that theory can analyse (asymptotic structure, scaling) and numerics can stress-test (convergence, sensitivity to truncation).
  • Theory ↔︎ experiment: The gauge fix provides a falsifiable target. Theory predicts \(K(t)\) under the Hayden–Sorce gauge from a model; experiment looks for the corresponding spectroscopic signature; disagreement is informative either way.
  • Numerics ↔︎ experiment: The pipeline produces predictions for specific platforms. Experiments produce fingerprints. Cross-platform consistency (or its absence) maps the regime where the construction is operationally useful.

The construction does not tell you, on its own, which gauge is “correct” in some deeper sense. That question requires either an experimental adjudication between gauges (which means finding a regime where their predictions visibly differ) or a theoretical argument that elevates one gauge above others. Both are open. The CBG construction makes the question tractable; it does not foreclose it.

9. Take-aways

Do not take away that \(K(t)\) is uniquely physical. It is gauge-conditional. Different principled gauges give different \(K\)’s.

Do take away that, once a gauge is fixed (here: Hayden–Sorce minimal dissipation), \(K(t)\) is computable, benchmarkable, and experimentally interrogable. It is a well-defined coordinate object, and a useful one.

Do internalise the distinction between a theoretical construction (which the gauge fix and Equation (7) provide), a numerical implementation (which the perturbative recursion enables), and an experimental fingerprint (which is platform-specific and one-of-many, until cross-platform replication accumulates). Each is necessary; none is sufficient.

10. Open questions an entry PhD student could engage with

These are not exhaustive. They are the questions where a student starting today could plausibly contribute within their thesis horizon.

  1. Convergence of the perturbative series in strong coupling. The Companion is explicit that convergence depends on system parameters, and that singularities can arise. Identifying the convergence radius for specific solvable models (Caldeira–Leggett, Fano–Anderson, Jaynes–Cummings, spin-boson) is a tractable numerical project with theoretical implications.
  2. Higher-order FDT structure. The second-order fluctuation–dissipation decomposition is striking. Does the structure persist at third or fourth order, in terms of higher-order response functions? This is an open theory question with an immediate numerical handle (the Companion gives explicit fourth-order expressions).
  3. Comparison of gauges in solvable models. Compute \(K(t)\) under both the Hayden–Sorce gauge and the Hamiltonian of mean force (or a polaron transform) in a model where the two are tractable — Fano–Anderson is the natural first venue. Identify a thermodynamic observable where they predict different things. This is a project that bridges theory, numerics, and (eventually) experiment.
  4. Independent experimental fingerprinting. The trapped-ion observation in Hasse et al. 2025 is a single platform. Reproducing the time-dependent renormalisation signature on a circuit-QED, NV-centre, neutral-atom, or photonic platform would substantially strengthen the empirical foundation. This is a project for someone in an experimental group with the right hardware.
  5. TCL singularity diagnostics. When the dynamical map fails to be invertible, the TCL approach loses its time-locality. Characterising precisely where and why this happens, and what physical phenomena correspond to such singularities, is a foundational question that combines theory and numerics.

If you are about to start a PhD in any of the three communities and are looking for a concrete entry point: the CBG construction sits at a productive junction. It is recent enough that not all questions are answered, mature enough that there is a clear scaffold, and clean enough that an entry student can compute meaningful things by hand within a few weeks of focused work.

11. Suggested reading order

If you have a few hours: read the Letter cover to cover. Skip the Companion’s derivations on a first pass; you can return to them.

If you have a week: work through the Letter, then the Companion’s Section II (the recursion) and Section IV (the explicit fourth-order expressions). Do the spin examples (§7 above) yourself; don’t just read them. The algebra is doable on a single sheet of paper.

If you have a month and a numerics inclination: implement Equation (7) for a small system of your choice (say, a two-level system coupled to a single bosonic mode), verify it against a textbook Lindblad limit, then run the recursion to compute \(K_2(t)\) and compare to a numerically exact propagation of the full system+bath dynamics. A repository is planned under the working name oqs-cbg-pipeline and is structured to support exactly this kind of project; it carries mandatory protective documentation (endorsement_marker.md, stewardship_conflict.md, do_not_cite_as.md) that governs how the code may be used and cited. Read those first if you fork the repository; the citation scope is narrower than a typical scientific code repository, and the protective files state the rules explicitly.

For background, the references on the project landing page are organised by purpose: textbooks (Breuer & Petruccione, Vacchini), gauge fix (Hayden & Sorce), non-Markovianity reviews (Rivas–Huelga–Plenio, Breuer–Laine–Piilo–Vacchini, Chruściński), strong-coupling thermodynamics, experimental fingerprints, and cross-method numerical references.

12. A closing remark on register

You will hear two sorts of language about \(K(t)\) in seminars over the next few years.

The first is reificatory: “the system Hamiltonian gets renormalised to \(K(t)\)”. This is convenient shorthand and you will catch yourself using it. It is also subtly misleading. \(K(t)\) is not the Hamiltonian of the open system in any frame-independent sense; it is the Hamiltonian in the Hayden–Sorce gauge.

The second is gauge-conscious: “under the minimal-dissipation gauge, the coherent part of the TCL generator corresponds to a Hermitian operator \(K(t)\) which we can compute, and which has time-dependent matrix elements.” This is more accurate and only marginally clunkier in spoken English.

You don’t have to be doctrinaire about it. But know which one you are doing, and notice when other people slip from one to the other. The papers themselves are careful; the seminar shorthand sometimes is not.

That is a small thing, and it is also the thing that distinguishes a clean PhD from a rushed one.

Good luck.
